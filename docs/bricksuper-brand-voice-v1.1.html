01 · Our Audience
Mum and dad investors — builders by nature

BrickSuper's audience is the Australian mum and dad investor. The tradie who owns their workshop. The GP who's been renting their clinic rooms for fifteen years. The couple who've built a small business from nothing and wonder if they're doing enough with their super.

They are not financial experts — and they're not trying to be. But they are builders by nature: people who understand that anything worth having takes time, effort, and the right foundations. They don't want jargon. They don't want pressure. They want someone to explain it straight, show them the risks alongside the rewards, and trust them to make the right call for their family.

They have worked hard for what they have. They deserve to understand what their super can do for them.

02 · The Brand Idea
Brick by brick
Not a slogan. A philosophy.

Property — especially commercial property — is not a get-rich-quick instrument. It is a foundation asset. One brick at a time, laid carefully, each one mortared in properly before the next goes down. That's how you build something that lasts. That's how mum and dad investors build financial security for retirement — not through speculation, but through steady, informed decisions made with the right knowledge and the right professionals beside them.

Every piece of content BrickSuper publishes should feel like it comes from someone who has laid a few bricks themselves. Who knows the difference between a solid foundation and a shaky one. Who will tell you when the ground isn't ready — even if that's not what you want to hear.

We talk about laying foundations, building security brick by brick, mortaring in the right decisions, making sure each layer is sound before the next goes down. We never talk about "turbocharging" super, "unlocking potential", or "getting started on a journey". We build. We don't sprint.

03 · Voice Pillars
Four principles, one voice
These four pillars govern every content decision — a 5,000-word guide or a two-line disclaimer. Each includes a plain-English test to apply before publishing.
Pillar 01
Plain-spoken
No jargon without explanation. No acronyms without expansion. We write the way a trusted friend who happens to know about SMSFs would talk — direct, warm, honest. When a technical term is unavoidable, we define it in the same sentence.
Would a couple sitting at their kitchen table on a Sunday evening understand this without stopping to Google anything?
Pillar 02
Balanced
Every benefit gets its corresponding risk. Every opportunity comes with the honest caveat. We're not here to scare people off — but we're not here to sell them a dream either. Mum and dad investors deserve the full picture so they can make a call that's right for their family.
If a reader acted on this and it went sideways, would they feel we misled them?
Pillar 03
Grounded
We cite the ATO. We reference legislation. We use real numbers. Credibility comes from specificity — "the ATO contacted 17,700 funds about concentration risk in 2019" lands differently from "some funds have been flagged". We show our working.
Is every factual claim either commonly verifiable, or linked to a primary source?
Pillar 04
Respectful
These are people who have built businesses, raised families, and made hard decisions all their lives. We never talk down to them, never panic them, never pressure them. We give them what they need to make their own call — and trust them to make it.
Does this treat the reader as a capable adult who is in control of their own future?

05 · Voice in Practice
Five scenarios, before and after
Real examples for the situations BrickSuper content writers encounter most often.
Explaining a complex concept
✅ Write this
"A limited recourse borrowing arrangement — an LRBA — lets your SMSF borrow money to buy a single asset, usually property. Think of it like the structural scaffold that lets you add a storey before it's fully paid off. 'Limited recourse' means if the loan goes bad, the lender can only claim the bare trust asset — not the rest of what your fund has built. That's the protection. The risk is the weight: an LRBA adds repayment pressure and concentration to a fund that may already be carrying both."
Defines the term plainly, uses a natural structural metaphor, pairs the protection with the risk immediately. No recommendation made.
❌ Not this
"LRBAs are a powerful tool savvy SMSF trustees use to turbocharge their portfolio. Ask our team how to make your super work harder."
Promotional framing, implies advice, "our team" implies in-house advice capacity, no risk mentioned.
Describing risk honestly
✅ Write this
"Putting 90% or more of your fund into one property is a bit like building your whole retirement on a single foundation stone. The ATO wrote to 17,700 trustees about exactly this in 2019. It's not illegal — but your investment strategy needs to explain in writing why it makes sense for your fund. That documentation is the mortar that holds it together if questions are ever asked."
Specific ATO citation, uses the foundation metaphor naturally, factual without being alarmist, directs to action without giving advice.
❌ Not this
"Some people worry about concentration, but with the right strategy your SMSF property investment can be a cornerstone of your retirement."
Dismisses a material compliance risk, implies BrickSuper can help find "the right strategy" — which is advice.
Presenting tool output
✅ Write this
"Based on what you've entered, your monthly repayment would be around $3,840 at current rates. Your projected rent is $3,200 — so there's a $640 gap each month the fund needs to cover. If rates rise 2%, that gap grows to $1,280. These are the load-bearing numbers to take to your adviser before anything else."
Purely mathematical, stress-test included, closes with an adviser referral framed as practical next step — not a recommendation.
❌ Not this
"Great news! Your fund looks well-placed for this property. Get connected with one of our specialists to lock this in."
Implies a suitability determination, uses urgency language, "lock this in" is pressure framing.
Connecting a reader with an adviser
✅ Write this
"If you'd like to sit down with a licensed SMSF specialist and work through the numbers properly, we can make that introduction. We earn a fixed referral fee for the connection — the same amount regardless of what advice you receive or what you decide to do next. The next move is always yours."
Transparent about the commercial arrangement, preserves autonomy, no pressure, no urgency.
❌ Not this
"Ready to take the next step? Our expert partners are standing by to help you get started on your SMSF property journey."
Urgency framing, "our experts" implies in-house advice, no fee disclosure, contains two banned phrases.
Flagging a high-risk scenario
✅ Write this
"What you've entered puts this property at 94% of your fund's assets after purchase. That's a lot of weight on one foundation stone. The ATO monitors funds at this concentration level, and your investment strategy will need to document — in writing, before you proceed — why that structure is right for your circumstances. A licensed adviser should review that before you go any further."
Uses the foundation metaphor naturally, factual, cites regulatory trigger, directs to required action without BrickSuper advising.
❌ Not this
"Your concentration is a little high, but many SMSF investors hold property as their main asset. Worth a chat with a professional."
Minimises a material compliance risk, vague about what action is actually required, provides false reassurance.
06 · Channel Guidance
Voice by channel
The core voice stays constant. Delivery adjusts by context.
Web Pillar content
  • Lead with what the reader needs to know — not a hook designed to pull them in
  • Subheadings answer questions directly: "What does 'wholly and exclusively' actually mean?"
  • Cite legislation by section number on first reference; plain English from there
  • Every benefit is followed by its corresponding risk
  • Close sections with "Questions to raise with your licensed adviser" — never advice
  • Brick-by-brick language where it fits naturally; never forced
Email Nurture sequence
  • Subject lines are specific and useful — not clickbait
  • One idea per email. Never bundle.
  • Opening line: the most valuable thing in the email — no pleasantries
  • Tone is a knowledgeable friend, not a financial firm's newsletter
  • Never personalise to the point it sounds like advice
  • Every email closes with the general information disclaimer
Tools UI copy
  • Labels describe what the field does — not what we want the user to feel
  • Output copy states what numbers mean, not what the reader should do
  • Risk flags are calm and steady: "This is worth pausing on" not "WARNING"
  • Disclaimer sits above the inputs, prominently — not buried below the output
  • "Before you build on this, here's what the numbers say" works well here
Downloads Lead magnets
  • Title: specific and functional — "Pre-Purchase Checklist — 32 items"
  • First page: full disclaimer before any content begins
  • CTAs inside: "Discuss this with your licensed adviser" — never "Contact us"
  • Abbreviated disclaimer in the footer of every page
  • Tone is a working document a builder would use on site — practical, no filler
07 · Compliance Mapping
Why the rules exist
Each compliance obligation maps directly to a voice decision. This is the legal framework behind every writing choice.
Compliance ruleWhat it means for how we write
ASIC RG 244 — factual information safe harbourWe state facts, not opinions. "The ATO safe harbour rate is 8.95%" — not "8.95% is a competitive rate."
Westpac v ASIC (2021 HCA 3) — disclaimers alone don't save youTool outputs must be substantively factual. The substance of what we say matters as much as the disclaimer on top of it.
s.923A — restricted termsNever use "independent", "impartial", or "unbiased" anywhere — not even in passing.
s.963L — conflicted remunerationDisclose the referral fee in plain language, before any lead form. Not in the footer. Before.
ASIC 26-029MR — pressure tacticsNo urgency language. No performance claims. No pressure in CTAs. This is how builders talk — not how salespeople talk.
SIS Act — sole purpose testAn SMSF exists to provide retirement benefits — not to hold property. Never imply otherwise.
08 · Pre-publish Checklist
Before you hit publish
Run every piece of content through this. When in doubt, escalate for compliance review.
Does it make any claim that could be read as financial advice?
Does it use any prohibited words or phrases?
Is every benefit paired with a corresponding risk or honest caveat?
Are all factual claims verifiable and cited where appropriate?
Does it treat the reader as a capable adult making their own decision?
Does it carry the right disclaimer for its context — page, tool, form, or download?
Does any CTA imply BrickSuper is recommending a course of action?
If there's a lead form, is the referral fee disclosed before submission?
Does any tool output read like a suitability determination?
Has a second reader checked for tone drift — anything that sounds promotional, urgent, or sales-driven?
Does the brick-by-brick language feel natural here, or forced? If forced, remove it.
A note on the metaphor

The brick-by-brick idea is powerful because it's true. Building financial security through property takes time, planning, and the right foundations. But like any good building metaphor — don't overdo it.

One well-placed reference per piece of content is enough. The goal is for it to feel like our natural way of seeing the world, not a marketing campaign we're running.

When it works, it works quietly. The reader feels it more than they notice it.

Questions about brand voice?

All content requires compliance sign-off before publication. Co-branded materials require brand team review. When in doubt, err on the side of factual — and refer the reader to a licensed adviser.
